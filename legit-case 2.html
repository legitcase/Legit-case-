What is Legit Case?

Legit Case is a courtroom role-playing game where opposing parties argue a fictional legal dispute. A judge evaluates both arguments and decides who presented the stronger, more persuasive case.

Each case is self-contained and focuses on reasoning, persuasion and strategy, not real legal knowledge. Real legal rules may only be applied if all three core players unanimously agree to adopt the same legal system before the case begins.

Draw case› Prepare› Argue› Question round› Verdict
All names, places and details in every case are entirely fictional. Any resemblance to real people or real events is purely coincidental.
Roles
Judge
Listens to both sides, asks questions in the Question Round, then delivers a verdict.
Required
Claimant
Brings the case to court. Argues first and sets the scene.
Required
Defendant
The party the case is brought against. Argues second and must counter the claimant.
Required
Witnesses
Provide testimony for whichever side called them. Played by extra players or by the claimant and defendant themselves.
Optional
Witnesses without extra players
If no additional players are available, the claimant and defendant may each assume the role of their own witnesses, stepping into that role briefly during their argument phase if they choose to call one.
Components
Case Card
One per case. The fictional dispute scenario shared by all players as context.
Fact Card
One per side per case. Each side's facts are different and support their own argument.
Witness List
One per side. Lists potential witnesses that player may call to testify.
Chaos Cards
6 disruptive effects earned by rolling a die during the opponent's argument phase.
Cases are organized in a fixed order so that each Case Card's Fact Cards and Witness Lists align correctly. Always draw them as a set and never mix cards from different cases.
How to Play
  • 1
    Draw a Case Card
    Reveal the Case Card. All players read the scenario. This is the shared context and backdrop for the entire case, and the Judge uses it to understand what is being disputed.
  • 2
    Distribute materials
    Each side receives their Fact Card and their Witness List. These are private and must not be shown to the opposing side or the Judge until used during arguments.
    Exception: the Side Change Chaos Card allows your opponent to view your Witness List.
  • 3
    Preparation phase
    Players prepare their arguments independently and in silence. You may argue on the spot without preparation if you prefer. If all three core players agree, you may adopt a specific legal system to apply to the case.
  • 4
    Argument phase
    The Claimant argues first, the Defendant second. Each player may call witnesses from their list at any point during their own argument phase. Time limits are optional and must be agreed on before the phase begins.
    Witness testimony does not count toward any time limit applied by a Chaos Card.
  • 5
    Chaos Card rolls
    While your opponent is arguing, you may roll the die up to 3 times per argument phase to attempt to earn a Chaos Card. See the Chaos Cards section for full rules.
  • 6
    Question Round Optional
    After both sides finish arguing, the Judge may ask each side up to 2 questions. Answers must be immediate with no preparation, no notes, no witnesses and no Chaos Cards. Agree before the case begins whether this round is in play.
    Each side may decline one question per round with no penalty.
  • 7
    Judge's decision
    The Judge deliberates and announces the winner. There are no ties. If a Question Round was played, how each side handled the questions is factored into the verdict.
Chaos Cards

Chaos Cards let you disrupt your opponent's argument. They add strategy and unpredictability, but you have to earn them.

3 sides marked ✕ (no card) and 3 marked ⚖ (Chaos Card awarded). Each player may roll up to 3 times per opponent argument phase, with a 15-second cooldown between rolls. The first roll may only happen after your opponent states their first fact.
Fact Off
Choose one fact your opponent stated. That fact is discarded and will not be considered by the Judge.
Timed Argument
Your opponent must finish their remaining argument within 1 to 2 minutes. Witness testimony is excluded.
Commit
Everything your opponent said is discarded. They restart using only 1 fact and 1 witness of their choice.
Switch
Your opponent must now argue to disprove your case rather than prove their own for the rest of their phase.
Side Change
Choose one witness from your opponent's list and move them to your side. If all have testified, flip one to support you instead.
First Witness is Lying
The first witness called is struck from the record. If none called yet, the first on their list is permanently removed.
Judging

After both argument phases are complete, the Judge runs the Question Round if in play, then deliberates and delivers a verdict.

Question Round (optional)
The Judge asks each side up to 2 questions after arguments are done. Answers must be immediate with no preparation, no notes, no witnesses and no Chaos Cards. Each side may decline one question per round with no penalty.
Criteria
  • Logic and coherence
  • Strength of facts used
  • Effective use of witnesses
  • Performance under Chaos
  • Answers under questioning
How to decide
  • Winner by strongest reasoning
  • Winner by survival through chaos
  • Winner by best answers under questioning
  • The verdict is the Judge's alone
Sudden Death — if the Judge cannot decide
There are no ties. If the Judge cannot reach a verdict, a Sudden Death Round is triggered. Each side delivers one final argument in under 30 seconds. The Judge decides immediately based solely on those two statements.
